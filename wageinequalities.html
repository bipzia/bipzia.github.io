Wage inequalities across countries

Introduction

This article deals with wage inequalities between women and men across countries. This subject has been debated for many years and most of the studies agreed on an imbalance between the wage of women and men. Over the past years, many countries led political actions to reduce this gap. In this article, we aim to provide additional descriptive statistics on the subject without discussing efficiency of these political actions.

OECD Analysis

Recently, OECD has published an analysis on the imbalance between the wage of women and men across OECD countries. The wage gap (in %) that they define as the difference between median earnings of men and women relative to median earnings of men is used to highlight inequalities. The study shows huge disparities between countries. In some countries the wage inequality is resonably low whereas in some others it is very high. For instance, in Korea in 2017 the median earnings of women was 34.6% below the one of men. In contrast, in 2014, the imbalance was only of 3.4% in Luxembourg. Over all OECD countries, this rate was established at 13.8% in 2016.

We understand that countries do not communicate every year the inequalities gap to the OECD for different reasons and, in our analysis, we don’t aim to challenge the OECD’s analysis. However, we will try to give further insights assuming basic hypothesis. These hypothesis will be developped in the next section and they may be challenged. Nevertheless, the main purpose of this article is to show how simple data anlysis and visual analysis may allow you to go further into any subject.

Framework

Data and data wrangling

In our analysis, we will use the dataset available on the OECD’s website. According to the OECD’s definition data refer to full-time employees on the one hand and to self-employed on the other. We will only focus on employees and we will reshape the dataset for the purpose of our study. This will lead use to a datasert with the inequalities gap (in %) per country and per year.

Hypothesis and predictions

For the sake of clarity, we decide to take very simple hypothesis. Thus, in order to predict the missing value we will assume there exists a linear relationship of inequalities gap over time. Furthermore, diving into the data, we can figure out that some countries have very few data. This is the case of Croatia for instance where we have only one value over the last four decades. We will not treat these value as far as our hypothesis on a linear relationshoip will lead us to assume there is no variation of inequalities gap for these countries that might be considered as unrealistic.

Also, we will keep only countries that have a significant linear relationship. In fact, most of them have a significant linear relation as far as they have enough data.
